Snakes of Western Australia

Western Australia is home to one of the world's richest snake faunas, with more than 100 species occurring across environments that range from the tropical Kimberley and Pilbara to the forests of the south-west and the vast deserts of the interior. The majority belong to the family Elapidae, which includes well-known venomous species such as dugites, tiger snakes, mulga snakes, death adders and numerous small desert specialists. Pythons are also represented, including the Carpet Python and Stimson's Python, while several species are found nowhere else on Earth. This remarkable diversity reflects Western Australia's immense size, varied climates and long geographic isolation. Although a handful of species are responsible for most encounters with people, many Western Australian snakes are rarely seen and remain poorly known, spending their lives hidden beneath leaf litter, in burrows or among remote rocky habitats.

Snake biology / reproduction

Snakes are highly specialised reptiles whose long, limbless bodies are supported by flexible vertebral columns and powerful muscles that allow efficient movement across a wide variety of habitats. Their forked tongues collect scent particles from the environment and deliver them to the Jacobson's organ in the roof of the mouth, giving snakes an exceptionally keen sense of smell. Most species are carnivorous and swallow prey whole using highly mobile skull bones and loosely connected jaws. Snake reproduction varies considerably between species: some lay eggs (oviparous), others give birth to live young (viviparous), while many retain eggs internally until they hatch shortly before birth (ovoviviparous). Courtship often involves males competing for access to females, and after mating the female may store sperm for extended periods before fertilisation occurs. Depending on the species, a clutch or litter may range from only a few offspring to more than a hundred, though parental care is generally limited once the young are born or hatch.

Snake venom

Snake venoms are complex mixtures of proteins and other compounds that have evolved primarily to immobilise and digest prey. Different venoms act in different ways: some attack the nervous system, others damage blood and tissues, while some affect muscles or interfere with blood clotting. In Australia, many medically significant snake bites produce few immediate local symptoms, making it important not to judge the severity of a bite by pain or swelling alone. Modern first aid emphasises the pressure-immobilisation technique, in which a firm bandage is applied over the bite site and the affected limb is immobilised to slow the movement of venom through the lymphatic system. The victim should be kept as still as possible and transported urgently to medical care. In hospital, diagnosis may involve venom detection and blood tests, and severe envenomation can often be treated effectively with antivenom and supportive care. Thanks to advances in first aid, antivenom and emergency medicine, fatalities from snakebite are now uncommon in Australia.

Snake ecology

Snakes play an important role in ecosystems as both predators and prey. By feeding on rodents, lizards, frogs, birds and other animals, they help regulate populations that might otherwise become overabundant, contributing to the balance of natural communities. In turn, snakes are an important food source for many predators, including birds of prey, monitor lizards, mammals and even other snakes. Different species occupy a wide variety of ecological niches, from burrowing species that spend most of their lives underground to arboreal snakes that hunt among branches and marine species that roam coastal waters. Because snakes are sensitive to changes in habitat quality, climate and prey availability, their presence and abundance can provide valuable indicators of ecosystem health. Despite their often secretive nature, snakes are integral components of food webs and perform ecological functions that benefit both natural environments and human communities.

Snake evolution

Snakes evolved from lizard-like ancestors during the Jurassic and Early Cretaceous periods, more than 100 million years ago. Over time, their bodies became increasingly elongated while their limbs were reduced and eventually lost, an adaptation thought to have aided movement through burrows, dense vegetation, and other confined environments. Fossils of early snakes show vestiges of hind limbs, providing evidence of their lizard ancestry. Modern genetic and anatomical studies place snakes within the squamate reptiles alongside lizards, with their closest living relatives thought to include monitor lizards and iguanians. Today, snakes have diversified into more than 4,000 species occupying habitats ranging from deserts and forests to oceans, making them one of the most successful groups of reptiles on Earth.

Sea snakes

Sea snakes (Hydrophiinae) – A subfamily of highly adapted marine elapid snakes found throughout the Indian and Pacific Oceans. Sea snakes evolved from terrestrial Australian elapid ancestors and are now fully or semi-aquatic, with many species spending their entire lives at sea. They possess paddle-shaped tails for efficient swimming, valved nostrils to prevent water intake, and specialised salt-excreting glands under the tongue to maintain osmotic balance in marine environments. Unlike most fish, sea snakes must still surface periodically to breathe air, although some species can remain submerged for extended periods during hunting and travel.

Hydrophis – A diverse genus of sea snakes widely distributed across tropical Indo-Pacific waters. Species within Hydrophis are among the most specialised marine snakes, often showing extreme body flattening and reduced ventral scutes to improve swimming efficiency. They typically prey on fish and eels in coral reef, estuarine, and coastal environments, using potent neurotoxic venoms adapted for rapid immobilisation in water.

Enhydrina – A genus of venomous sea snakes known commonly as beaked sea snakes. Members of Enhydrina are characterised by a distinctive hook-shaped snout and are among the more aggressive sea snake species when provoked. They inhabit shallow coastal waters and estuaries across the Indian Ocean and western Pacific, where they hunt fish using highly efficient neurotoxic venom systems.

Laticauda – A genus of semi-aquatic sea snakes commonly known as sea kraits. Unlike fully marine sea snakes, Laticauda species retain functional ventral scales and spend significant time on land, where they come ashore to digest prey, rest, and reproduce. They are found throughout the Indo-Pacific region and represent an evolutionary intermediate between terrestrial elapids and fully marine sea snakes.

Pelamis platura – The pelagic sea snake, also known as the yellow-bellied sea snake, and one of the most widely distributed snake species on Earth. It is fully oceanic, drifting with currents across the Indo-Pacific and occasionally entering temperate waters. It has a strongly flattened tail for swimming, a highly buoyant body, and feeds primarily on small fish near the ocean surface.

Venom adaptation in sea snakes – Sea snake venoms are among the most potent of any reptiles, typically dominated by neurotoxins that rapidly immobilise fish prey in aquatic environments where escape is easy. Many species show venom variation depending on geography and diet, and their venom systems are closely related to those of Australian terrestrial elapids, reflecting their evolutionary origin on the Australian continental shelf.

Cultural significance of snakes

Snakes have played a complex and enduring role in human culture throughout history, symbolising both danger and healing, death and renewal. In ancient civilisations such as Egypt, Greece and India, snakes were often associated with divine power and rebirth due to their ability to shed their skin. The Rod of Asclepius, still used today as a symbol of medicine, reflects the Greek association of snakes with healing and medical knowledge. In contrast, Judeo-Christian traditions frequently portray snakes as symbols of temptation and deceit, most famously in the story of the Garden of Eden. Across Indigenous cultures in Australia, snakes also appear in Dreaming stories, often as powerful ancestral beings linked to creation, water and landforms. This dual symbolism—both revered and feared—has persisted into modern times, where snakes continue to evoke fascination, respect and caution in equal measure.

In many Aboriginal Australian cultures, the Rainbow Serpent is one of the most significant beings in Dreaming stories, often described as a powerful ancestral snake that shaped the land during creation. As it moved across the landscape, it is said to have carved out rivers, waterholes and valleys, bringing life and structure to the world while also embodying the forces of water, rain and fertility. In some traditions it is a creator and protector of life, while in others it can be a dangerous force that must be respected and appeased. The Rainbow Serpent is not a single uniform figure but varies widely between language groups and regions, reflecting the diversity of Aboriginal cultures across Australia. Today, it remains an important spiritual symbol connecting people to Country, law and the continuing presence of ancestral forces in the natural world.

Extinct snakes

Titanoboa – An extinct genus of giant snake from the Paleocene epoch, discovered in Colombia. Titanoboa cerrejonensis is the largest known snake to have ever existed, reaching estimated lengths of around 12–14 metres and weighing close to a tonne. It lived in warm, tropical swamp environments shortly after the extinction of the dinosaurs, occupying the role of apex predator in its ecosystem, likely feeding on large fish, crocodilians, and other sizable vertebrates. Its discovery demonstrated that prehistoric equatorial climates could support far larger ectothermic reptiles than are seen today.

Madtsoiidae (including Wonambi) – An extinct family of large constricting snakes that lived across Gondwanan continents including Australia, South America, Africa, and India. Members of this group, such as Wonambi naracoortensis, survived in Australia until relatively recent geological times (possibly into the late Pleistocene). These snakes were non-venomous ambush predators, likely resembling oversized modern pythons in ecology and behaviour, and may have coexisted with early human populations in Australia.

Gigantophis – An extinct genus of large snake from the Eocene epoch of North Africa, particularly Egypt. Gigantophis garstini is estimated to have reached lengths of around 9–11 metres, making it one of the largest known snakes in its time. It likely inhabited warm, wet environments and functioned as a constrictor-style predator, occupying a top or near-top position in its ecosystem.

Palaeophis – An extinct genus of prehistoric marine snakes from the Eocene epoch. Species of Palaeophis were adapted to aquatic life and may have reached lengths of up to 9 metres. Unlike most modern snakes, they occupied coastal and marine environments, likely moving in an eel-like manner and feeding on fish and other marine organisms. They represent one of the few known examples of large-bodied marine-adapted snakes in the fossil record.

Caudal autotomy (rare in snakes, but relevant contextually) – The ability of some reptiles to voluntarily shed part of the tail to escape predators; while true autotomy is not present in snakes, some species may still exhibit tail damage survival strategies and regenerative scarring patterns that are studied comparatively.

Cryptic coloration – Camouflage patterns and colouration that reduce detectability by prey or predators, often matching leaf litter, sand, bark, or soil substrates in specific habitats.

Fossorial adaptation – Morphological and behavioural traits associated with burrowing life, including reduced eye size, reinforced skull structure, and streamlined bodies suited for movement through soil or sand.

Hemotoxin – A class of venom components that disrupt blood coagulation, destroy red blood cells, or damage vascular tissue, often producing systemic effects rather than immediate neurological symptoms.

Myotoxin – Venom peptides that target skeletal muscle tissue, causing necrosis or severe muscle breakdown, sometimes leading to secondary kidney damage due to myoglobin release.

Proteroglyphous dentition – A fang arrangement characteristic of elapids, where short, fixed front fangs deliver venom through a grooved or partially enclosed canal rather than hinged folding fangs.

Opisthoglyphous dentition – A rear-fanged system in which enlarged posterior maxillary teeth assist in envenomation, typically requiring a chewing action to deliver venom effectively.

Saltation locomotion – A rare locomotor mode in certain desert-dwelling snakes involving rapid, jerky movements across unstable substrates such as sand or loose gravel, minimising surface contact and heat transfer.

Thermoregulatory shuttling – Behaviour in which snakes repeatedly move between sun-exposed and shaded microhabitats to maintain optimal body temperature for metabolism, digestion, and embryonic development.

Viperid-style strike kinematics (comparative term) – The study of rapid strike mechanics involving acceleration, fang deployment timing, and neuromuscular control, often used in comparative physiology even when describing non-viper species.

Venom ontogeny – Developmental changes in venom composition as a snake matures, often resulting in differences between juvenile and adult toxin profiles.

Ventral scutellation – The pattern and count of enlarged ventral scales used in taxonomic identification and phylogenetic studies, particularly important in distinguishing closely related species.

Zygapophyseal flexibility – The interlocking articulation of vertebrae that allows extreme lateral flexibility while maintaining spinal stability during locomotion and constriction.

Serpentine – From Latin serpentinus (“snake-like”), derived from serpens meaning snake; used to describe something winding, twisting, or snake-shaped.

Serpent – From Latin serpens (“creeping thing, snake”), from the verb serpere meaning “to crawl”; historically the general word for snake in learned English.

Ophidian – From Greek ophis (ὄφις), meaning “snake”; used in scientific and literary English as an adjective meaning “snake-like” or “of snakes.”

Ophiology – From Greek ophis (“snake”) + -logia (“study of”); the study of snakes (now usually subsumed under herpetology).

Ophidiophobia – From Greek ophis (“snake”) + phobos (“fear”); an irrational fear of snakes.

Dragon – From Greek drakōn (“serpent” or “giant snake”), originally referring to large serpentine creatures before later evolving into mythological dragons.

Draco – From Latin draco (“serpent, dragon”), used in scientific names and historical texts, also the root of words related to dragon-like serpents.

Viper – From Latin vipera (often analysed as vivus “live” + parere “to give birth”), originally referring to venomous snakes; though not “snake” as a root, it became a primary English word for venomous snakes.

Hydra – From Greek hydra (“water serpent”), a multi-headed serpent in mythology; etymologically tied to ancient serpent imagery in Greek myth.

Naga – From Sanskrit nāga, meaning “serpent” or “serpent deity”; appears in English through religious and mythological borrowing, especially in South Asian contexts.

Python – From Greek mythology (Pythōn), a serpent slain by Apollo; later applied to large constricting snakes, reinforcing the serpent association.

Serpiginous – From Latin serpere (“to creep, crawl”), meaning spreading in a winding, snake-like pattern; often used in medical contexts to describe lesion growth.

Insinuate – From Latin insinuare (“to wind into a narrow space”), originally from sinu (“curve, fold”), used metaphorically for something creeping in like a snake through a gap; now means to suggest indirectly or subtly.

Sinister – From Latin sinister (“left”); in Roman omen-reading, the left side was associated with ill fortune and serpentine symbolism in some Indo-European traditions, later developing into the modern sense of something evil or threatening.

Sinuous – From Latin sinuosus (“full of bends”), from sinu- (“fold, curve”), originally describing winding, snake-like motion; now means curving or having many turns.

Sinus – From Latin sinus (“curve, fold, bay”), originally meaning a hollow or bent space; the same root that produced “sinuous,” reflecting curved or coiled shapes reminiscent of serpentine motion.

Intestine – From Latin intestinus (“internal”), from in- + testis; in some historical Indo-European metaphor systems, internal winding structures (like intestines) were linguistically compared to coiled or serpentine shapes, though the etymology is anatomical rather than literal snake-based.

Snake – From Old English snaca, from Proto-Germanic *snakô, from a Proto-Indo-European root meaning “to crawl, creep.” This is the core descendant term.

Sneak – From Middle English sneken, from Proto-Germanic forms related to snakô, originally meaning “to creep or move stealthily like a crawling creature.”

Snail – From Old English snægl, from Proto-Germanic *snagilaz, from the same Indo-European “creep/crawl” root, referring to a slow crawling animal.

Snaky – From Old English snaca (“snake”) + adjectival suffix. Literally “like a snake,” preserving the direct morphological connection to the animal root.

Snakeweed / snake-like plant names (compound derivatives) – Botanical compounds using “snake” directly inherit the same Old English root snaca, extending the semantic field into descriptive morphology (coiling, creeping growth).

Naga – From Sanskrit nāga (“serpent being”). Entered English through religious, anthropological, and mythological writing about South Asia; used to describe divine serpent beings in Hindu and Buddhist traditions.

Naag / Nagini – Extended Sanskrit-derived forms appearing in English fiction, religious texts, and cultural borrowing (e.g., “Nagini” in South Asian mythology and modern fantasy literature).

Boa – From Latin boa, originally used by Romans for a large serpent (possibly thought to suffocate cattle). Entered English via zoological classification (boas).

Anaconda – From Sinhalese (Sri Lankan) henakandaya, meaning a type of large snake. Entered English via colonial natural history descriptions in the 18th century.

Viper – From Latin vipera, widely used in English for venomous snakes. Entered through Old French into Middle English.

Cobra – From Portuguese cobra de capelo (“snake with hood”), shortened to “cobra.” Entered English via colonial contact in India.

Mamba – From African languages (likely Bantu origins). Entered English via zoological classification of African venomous snakes.

Taipan – From the Dharug language of the Sydney region in Australia. The word was recorded in the 19th century and entered Australian English through early colonial natural history accounts. It was adopted as the common name for several highly venomous elapid snakes in the genus Oxyuranus, including the inland taipan (Oxyuranus microlepidotus), one of the most venomous snakes in the world. In English usage, “taipan” has become a standard zoological term rather than a purely local Indigenous word, reflecting a broader pattern in Australian English where Aboriginal language names were incorporated into scientific classification and common nomenclature for native fauna.
