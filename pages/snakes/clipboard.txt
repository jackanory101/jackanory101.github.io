During the Great Depression in the 1930s, the WA government and local road boards actually placed cash bounties on venomous snakes to encourage eradication around developing suburbs. Schoolchildren around the swampy fringes of the metropolitan area would skip class to go "snake hunting" in the paperbarks, using long sticks or slingshots. They would bring the tails of tiger snakes into local municipal offices to collect a bounty of a few pence per snake—which was decent pocket money at the time.
​The practice was eventually stopped because hospital admissions for children experiencing tiger snake bites skyrocketed, proving that paying kids to corner highly venomous elapids was a bit of a public health oversight
